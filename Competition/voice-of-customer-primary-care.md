## TL;DR — answers to the brief

1. **Did the wait-time hypothesis hold?** Partially, and it's more nuanced than "yes/no." Wait time is a real, well-documented, *worsening* pain nationally and locally — but survey data suggests it's more often the thing that makes people **switch** or **complain** once they're already looking, not the single most common reason a search **starts**. The two most-cited actual triggers in named surveys are (a) insurance/network disruption and (b) a doctor retiring, leaving, or simply not taking new patients — both of which then *express themselves* as a wait-time problem the moment the patient tries to book. So: wait time is real and urgent, but it's the symptom you'll be judged on, not necessarily the search query that starts the journey.
2. **Top 3 pains, ranked, with evidence strength:**
   - **(Strong, national + local verbatim) Feeling rushed / not truly known by your doctor.** 68% of patients report feeling rushed "sometimes or always"; 86% say no doctor sees their full picture across providers; local reviews of Mission Heritage and Optum describe two-second PA visits and doctors who "glance at the chart as if meeting them for the first time." This is the pain MedGlo's own existing patient testimonial already answers verbatim (see below) — the strongest, most provable asset in this whole file.
   - **(Strong, national + local verbatim) Can't get in when you actually need to be seen / can't get anyone on the phone.** National family-medicine new-patient wait now averages 23.5 days (up from 20.6 in 2022); California's own timely-access rate is 53.6% and falling; locally, verbatim reviews describe being told to "wait until May" for bloodwork, going "almost two months" without a callback, and being denied scheduling without a referral already in hand.
   - **(Moderate, structural not verbatim) Insurance-driven forced switching and "ghost network" confusion.** 42% say they chose their last doctor because of insurance-plan match; up to ~24% of California primary-care directory listings have been found inaccurate — meaning many searches start with several dead-end calls before a patient reaches a real, in-network, new-patient-accepting doctor.
3. **The single biggest thing that would change how the funnel gets built:** MedGlo has almost no independent, third-party-verifiable patient reviews for primary care yet (only two self-published testimonials on the practice's own site). Before spending on any channel, the highest-value, lowest-cost move is to ask Andrea/Dr. Recalde what patients *actually say at intake* about why they called — that internal, already-existing data will answer Josh's question faster and more reliably than any external research pass, this one included.

## 1. The trigger — what actually makes a South-OC adult go looking for a new PCP

Ranked by how often it appears in named survey/data sources. I could not find a South-Orange-County-specific survey of trigger reasons — everything below is national or California-state data, cross-checked against local verbatim reviews where possible. Flagged **(local evidence)** where a South-OC-specific data point supports it, **(national only)** where it doesn't.

1. **Insurance/network disruption — new job, new plan, employer switch, or "your old doctor just isn't in-network anymore."** 42% of patients say they picked their *last* doctor because that doctor was on their insurance plan — implying the same logic drives the next switch when the plan changes ([Physicians Practice, via US News survey summary](https://health.usnews.com/health-care/top-doctors/articles/primary-care-experiences-survey-report)). This is compounded locally by "ghost networks": California directories have been found inaccurate for roughly a quarter of primary-care listings, meaning a plan-driven search often fails on the first few calls ([CalMatters, Oct 2025](https://calmatters.org/health/2025/10/health-insurance-error-denial-ghost-network/); [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/ghost-networks-health-insurance-regulators)). **(national only, but structurally very plausible for South OC's white-collar, high-job-mobility population — inferred, not proven locally.)**
2. **The old doctor retired, left the group, or simply "isn't taking new patients."** In a survey of people actively searching for a new PCP, 20% said their prior doctor had retired or moved ([US News survey summary](https://health.usnews.com/health-care/top-doctors/articles/primary-care-experiences-survey-report)). **(local evidence)** — directly echoed in a Mission Viejo-based review: *"Good luck trying to get an appointment with this group. Most of their doctor's are not taking new patients."* (Nancy Egan, 2★, Mission Heritage Medical Group, via Birdeye).
3. **Acute/reactive need that won't wait — sick now, need a referral, need a refill, need labs.** This is the pain that most closely maps to Josh's hypothesis. It's well documented as a reason people **switch** (about 20% of switchers cite delays specifically, per continuity-of-care literature — [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11936363/)) more than as the reason a search **starts fresh**, but for someone with no PCP at all (9–14% of adults, per PartnerMD's 2026 survey), an acute need is very often the *entire* trigger — they had no doctor, got sick or needed a form filled out, and are now looking for the first time. **(national data; local verbatim below confirms the pain exists here too, just doesn't prove it's the *first* trigger.)**
4. **Milestone/preventive moments — turning 40/50, a work or school physical, a Medicare wellness visit, or wanting help with weight loss (GLP-1).** ~30% of adults say a specific health-related need or expertise area drives their doctor choice ([US News summary](https://health.usnews.com/health-care/top-doctors/articles/primary-care-experiences-survey-report)); GLP-1 demand is a genuinely massive and growing category nationally (roughly 1 in 8 Americans has used a GLP-1 medication; ~129 million estimated to qualify) — but I found **no local or national evidence that GLP-1 interest is currently driving people to seek a *new primary care doctor* specifically**, as opposed to a med spa, telehealth GLP-1 service, or their existing PCP. Flag as an emerging, unproven-as-a-PCP-acquisition-driver trend, not a confirmed trigger. **(thin — do not treat as validated.)**
5. **New diagnosis or a chronic condition that needs ongoing management.** Backed by general literature on fragmented chronic-disease care (47% of Americans with a chronic illness report fragmented care) but I found **no South-OC-specific verbatim evidence** of this as an initiating trigger. **(thin, inferred.)**
6. **Relocation into the area.** Plausible given South OC's move-up-buyer demographics, but I found zero direct evidence (survey or review) quantifying this as a trigger here. **(unproven — flagged, not claimed.)**

## 2. The pain, in their words

### National survey data (not South-OC-specific, but named, dated, and citable)
From PartnerMD's **2026 State of Primary Care Report** (survey of 500+ patients; note — PartnerMD sells concierge medicine, so this survey has a plausible incentive to make traditional primary care look bad; treat as directional, not gospel):
- **62%** wait a week or more to see their doctor, or don't have a primary care physician at all.
- **68%** report feeling rushed during appointments "sometimes" or "always."
- **76%** wait 10+ minutes past their scheduled time once they arrive.
- **62%** cannot reach their doctor's office after hours.
- **86%** say their doctor doesn't consistently see the full picture across the other providers they see.
- **80%** worry an important health issue could slip through the cracks.
- Only **14%** feel very confident they're getting the best possible care; overall NPS is **-52**.
- **22%** wish their doctor spent more time explaining results and options; **21%** want more time on prevention; **17%** want more time coordinating care and listening.
([PartnerMD 2026 State of Primary Care Report](https://www.partnermd.com/state-of-primary-care-2026))

National new-patient wait times (**AMN Healthcare, 2025 survey of 15 major U.S. metros** — not South-OC-specific, and OC is not broken out):
- Average new-patient wait across all specialties: **31 days**, up 19% since 2022 and 48% since 2004.
- **Family medicine specifically: 23.5 days**, up from 20.6 days in 2022.
([AMN Healthcare](https://www.amnhealthcare.com/amn-insights/physician/whitepapers/2025-survey-of-physician-appointment-wait-times/); [GlobeNewswire summary](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2025/05/27/3088705/0/en/New-Survey-Shows-Physician-Appointment-Wait-Times-Surge-19-Since-2022-48-Since-2004.html))

California-specific (state dashboard; **data is from 2021, the underlying survey was discontinued/"sunset" in 2024**, so this is the most authoritative number available but it is stale and statewide, not South-OC):
- Only **53.6%** of patients received primary outpatient care within the state's own timely-access standard (10–15 business days for non-urgent care) in 2021 — down from 58.7% in 2016.
([Let's Get Healthy California](https://letsgethealthy.ca.gov/goals/redesigning-the-health-system/increasing-timely-care/))

## 4. Search and discovery behaviour

What I found (national data, from Zocdoc's own **"What Patients Want" 2025/2026 annual report**, built from millions of real Zocdoc bookings — the most robust behavioral dataset located in this pass):
- Patients compare an average of **21 distinct provider profiles** before booking (more for psychologists/OB-GYNs, fewer for urgent care).
- **84%** return to the same provider next time once they find a fit — so the "shopping" phase, while real, is a one-time cost per relationship, not a recurring habit.
- **Personal connection** is the #1 decision factor — ranked twice as important as proximity or scheduling convenience; **proximity** is #2; a **highly rated doctor** is #3.
- **52%** book within 4 days of deciding they need care; **22%** book next-day; **43%** of bookings happen outside office hours (evenings/weekends) — i.e., people are often searching and booking on their own time, not during a 9-5 window.
- **93%** of appointments (excluding mental health) are in-person, even among Gen Z — this is not a telehealth-first category.
- **92%** of bookings stay in-network — insurance match is a hard filter, not a preference.
- The specific language that shows up disproportionately in 5-star reviews: *"listens carefully," "like family," "extremely comfortable," "thoughtful questions"* — this maps almost exactly onto MedGlo's own existing testimonial wording (see Section 6).
([Zocdoc: What Patients Actually Look for When Choosing a Doctor in 2026](https://www.zocdoc.com/resources/blog/article/patients-choosing-doctor-2026))

General guidance sources (Medical Board of California, Kaiser's own patient guidance, Quora threads) consistently describe the same **discovery sequence**: check the insurance-plan's own "find a doctor" directory first → ask people you already trust (friends, family, your dentist or pharmacist) → check ratings on Google/Zocdoc/Healthgrades → book. Given the ghost-network problem documented above, the first step (insurance directory) is disproportionately likely to fail or frustrate before a patient ever reaches step two or three.

## 5. The Spanish-speaking segment

**Population context:** Mission Viejo is roughly 19.8–20% Hispanic/Latino by the latest Census ACS estimates ([Census QuickFacts](https://www.census.gov/quickfacts/fact/table/missionviejocitycalifornia/PST045224); [Neilsberg](https://www.neilsberg.com/insights/mission-viejo-ca-population-by-race/)) — a meaningful minority, not a majority, of the immediate city (South OC overall skews somewhat lower than broader Orange County).

**Real, directly-verified evidence (first-party, from MedGlo's own site — confirmed via direct fetch, not search-summary):** MedGlo's primary-care landing page already carries a patient testimonial: *"Dr. Recalde is bilingual which is perfect for my mom. The office is beautiful and care is exceptional."* — Alyssa A. This is genuine and important: it suggests the actual buying pattern is often an **adult child booking/vouching for a Spanish-preferring parent**, not necessarily a Spanish-speaking adult booking for themselves. That's a specific, usable nuance if this pattern holds up (it's one testimonial; I could not independently verify it on Google/Yelp — see Source Reliability Notes).

**Supporting (not local) evidence:** a systematic literature review found that language concordance between doctor and Spanish-preferring patients meaningfully improves the primary-care patient experience ([Journal of Racial and Ethnic Health Disparities](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40615-024-01951-z)) — this is real academic support that bilingual care is a genuine trust/quality factor, not just a "nice to have," for patients who prefer Spanish.

**Complication:** bilingual staff/care is **already advertised by at least one strong nearby competitor** — MemorialCare Medical Group in Laguna Hills (4.9★) lists Spanish, Hindi, Punjabi, and Farsi among staff languages. So "we speak Spanish" alone is not obviously a unique differentiator in this specific market; it may be closer to table stakes at the better-run competitors.

**One suggestive, not conclusive, data point:** a 1-star Mission Heritage review alleged front-desk rudeness "with an attitude towards any kind of accent" — a single, unverified complaint, but it is a real quote that hints some competitors' front-of-house may not feel warm toward non-native-English speakers. I would not build a claim on one review, but it's directionally consistent with an opening for "warm, unhurried, no attitude" as a positioning layer for this segment.

**Honest bottom line:** the evidence that Spanish is *decisive* (vs. a nice-to-have layer) for primary care specifically is **real but thin** — one first-party testimonial, one general academic finding, no independent local forum/review discussion establishing unmet Spanish-language primary-care demand as its own category. This is consistent with (but does not, on its own, prove for primary care) the aesthetics-side finding that Spanish functions as warmth rather than a cold hook.

## 6. Which of MedGlo's real assets map to real pain

- **Same physician every visit, unhurried, unhurried explanation — likely the single strongest asset found in this whole pass.** It directly answers the most consistently documented national pain (68% feel rushed, 86% say no one sees the full picture, NPS -52) and is already backed by a real, first-party MedGlo testimonial: *"Dr. Recalde is amazing! She takes her time, explains everything thoroughly, and genuinely cares."* — Aileen S. This same language ("listens carefully," "extremely comfortable") is exactly what Zocdoc's data says separates 5-star reviews from average ones. **Decisive**, and already provable.
- **Insurance accepted, no membership fee.** Given that concierge/DPC alternatives already exist and are already being paid for in this exact catchment (Coastal Concierge, Kolvita, Morris Hasson, Portola, Hoag Concierge), MedGlo's ability to offer a similar unhurried/continuity experience *without* a membership fee, on top of accepted insurance, is a genuinely differentiated, provable claim versus that specific competitive set. **Likely decisive versus the concierge segment; table stakes versus Kaiser/Optum/MemorialCare (who also take insurance).**
- **Fast new-patient access (site claims 1–2 weeks).** If operationally true, this beats the documented 23.5-day national family-medicine average and directly answers the verbatim local complaints ("wait until May," "almost two months," "not taking new patients"). This needs an internal, honest check — is it actually true today, at Dr. Recalde's real capacity, alongside her aesthetics patients and the goal of 5 visits/day? If true, it's a strong, quotable claim; if not reliably true, it would backfire immediately given how sharply patients punish broken access promises (per the reviews above). **Potentially decisive — verify before using.**
- **Direct phone line, small practice, a person answers.** Not itself compelling, but it directly contrasts with documented local failure modes ("tried contacting the office for almost two months and couldn't get a call back"; "one week I still have not heard back"). **Table stakes that competitors are visibly failing at — worth having, not worth leading with.**
- **Bilingual (EN/ES).** A credible trust/warmth layer, especially for multigenerational households booking care for a Spanish-preferring parent — but not proven decisive on its own, and at least one strong competitor already offers it. **Likely a nice-to-have/warmth layer, not a standalone hook — consistent with, though not independently proven by, the aesthetics-side finding.**
- **Adults-only, 18+, no pediatrics.** No evidence gathered either way on whether this helps or hurts; it simplifies operations but I found nothing suggesting patients are actively seeking an "adults-only" practice as a benefit. **Unproven, likely neutral.**
- **GLP-1/medical weight loss in a PCP setting.** A large and growing national category, but **no evidence found that it currently drives people to seek a new PCP specifically** in this market (versus a med spa, telehealth service, or their existing doctor). **Worth tracking as an emerging angle, not yet a proven driver.**
- **Mental health medication management.** No evidence gathered either way. **Unknown.**

## 7. Channel

Based on everything above, paid social (interruption-based) is structurally a poor fit for how people actually find a primary care doctor, and this matches what MedGlo already observed: a Traffic-objective Meta campaign generated 931 landing-page views at $0.10 each and 975 clicks — with zero leads. That's consistent with the evidence in this file, not a coincidence:
- Choosing a PCP is a **research-heavy, insurance-filtered, trust-building decision** (21 providers compared on average; personal connection ranked #1; 92% stay in-network) — not an impulse category the way a cosmetic treatment can be.
- The **discovery sequence patients themselves describe** starts with the insurance directory, then word-of-mouth/referral, then rating sites — none of which is paid social.
- **93% of visits happen in person** and the decision is trust-based, meaning the win condition is being *found and trusted* at the moment someone is already looking (search, insurer directory, Google Business Profile, Zocdoc/Healthgrades), not being interrupted while scrolling.

Plainly: the evidence points toward **being findable and credible at the point of intent** (accurate/complete insurance-directory listings across every accepted carrier, a strong Google Business Profile specifically for the primary-care service line, a complete and accurate Zocdoc/Healthgrades profile, and independent Google reviews from actual primary-care patients) as a structurally better-fitting starting point than paid social — and **mining MedGlo's existing aesthetics patient base** (people who already trust Dr. Recalde personally) as a warm, zero-CAC channel that directly leverages the #1 decision factor found in this research (personal connection). I did not evaluate paid-search cost-per-click economics (no keyword-volume tool access — see gap above), so I can't say whether Google Ads/PPC would be efficient; that's a distinct open question from whether paid *social* fits, which the evidence says it does not.

## Source reliability notes

- **WebSearch** results in this environment return an AI-generated summary of search snippets, not always a verbatim original — wherever a quote below came only from a WebSearch summary rather than a direct WebFetch of the page, I've noted it as "aggregated"/"paraphrase-level" rather than presenting it as a confirmed exact quote.
- **Yelp direct-fetch was blocked (HTTP 403) on every attempt** in this pass; where Yelp data appears, it came via WebSearch snippets of Yelp pages or via Birdeye, which aggregates Google reviews for the same business and was fetchable directly in most cases.
- **Birdeye** was the most reliable route to verbatim Google reviews and was used for most of the quoted competitor complaints above; note it typically only surfaces a handful of "top"/recent reviews per fetch, not the full archive.
- **Reddit** was inaccessible to both search and direct fetch in this pass (no results returned for site:reddit.com queries; no direct URLs found to fetch) — flag this as an evidence gap, not an absence of discussion.
- **PissedConsumer and BBB** (used for Hoag) are self-selected, extreme-complaint venues — useful for surfacing worst-case failure modes (billing, rudeness) but not representative of average sentiment.
- **PartnerMD's "2026 State of Primary Care Report"** is a survey commissioned by a company that sells concierge medicine — directionally useful and independently cited elsewhere, but has a plausible incentive to make traditional/insurance-based primary care look worse than it may be. Treated as a national signal, not a verified local number.
- **AMN Healthcare's wait-time survey** covers 15 major U.S. metro areas; Orange County is not separately broken out. Treated as a national benchmark only.
- **California's 53.6% timely-access figure** is the most authoritative state-level number found, but its source survey was discontinued ("sunset") in 2024 and the figure itself is from 2021 — it is stale and statewide, not South-OC-specific.
- **No Ahrefs/Semrush/Google Keyword Planner/Search Console access was available** in this environment — every "search behavior" claim in this file is behavioral/survey-based (mainly Zocdoc's own reported booking data), not search-volume-based. This is a genuine gap for anyone making a channel-budget decision off this file.
- **MedGlo's own two testimonials** (Aileen S., Alyssa A.) were confirmed by direct WebFetch of `med-glo.com/primary-care/` — the wording above is exact as pulled from the live page on 2026-07-21, not a search-engine paraphrase. They are, however, self-published/curated by the practice, not independently verifiable third-party reviews (no matching reviews were found on Google/Yelp/Birdeye in this pass).
